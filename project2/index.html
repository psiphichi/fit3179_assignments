GDP Growth Rate

This site shows global economic data with visualisations and provides some insights into it. Let's first take a look at the map below. The map shows the annual GDP growth rate in a percentage based on the price in 2005 by country. Although this data itself may be slightly outdated, it will still give an idea of which countries tend to have higher/lower growth rates. For example, developing countries in Asia and Africa tend to have higher growth rate, while developed countries such as Western European nations have lower values. Use the bottom left slider and the map centre drop-down to select the centre and zoom in.

Population Density

Now, let's have a look at the following map, which shows the population density per km2 in 2017 by country. By comparing with the first map, you can see that the population density and the GDP growth rates has a positive correlation to some extent. The notable examples are Ethiopia, China and India. This observation should be reasonable as the population growth will usually increase the production within that given nation, which means a rise in GDP.

Relations of Population, GDP, CO2 Emission

Here are scatterplot matrix showing the correlations of the population, GDP, and CO2 emission by country. These 3 attributes have been chosen based on the correlation analysis on the original data, which revealed that these 3 have high correlations to each other. At a glance, the charts may seem cluttered and do not show high correlations, but you may zoom in to take a closer look at them. By doing so, you should be able to realise the correspondence between those 3. The bottom left filter can be used to see the values based on different regions to observe the differences between them.
To have a clearer insight, let's take a look at each of the 3 attributes separately. 2 charts right below show the top countries by population and GDP respectively.

Population by Country

GDP by Country

As you can see, China, USA, India, Brazil, Russia, Mexico and Japan are in both 2 charts. By paying attention to the regions of those countries, you can see that African countries with a large populations are not in the GDP chart, which shows the relatively low standard of their economic status. On contrary, none of the Western European nations in the GDP chart is in the population chart, which is a reflection of the high productivity in those countries.

CO2 Emission by Country

(million tons/tons per capita)

This chart on the left shows the top countries by the amount of CO2 emission. Here, you can see that countries with a large amount of CO2 emission are either ones with large populations or oil rich-ones. It is notable that China is producing by far larger amount of emissions compared to India, at least more than 4 times larger, even though the population is almost the same. Russia and Saudi Arabia are ranked high primarily because of their large production of oil and other resources. Again, European nations except Russia tend to have high GDPs with smaller emissions, and this can be explained by their strict eco-friendly policies led by the EU.
